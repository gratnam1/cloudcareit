Why Most Microsoft 365 Tenants Are Misconfigured

Microsoft 365 ships in a permissive default state. Legacy authentication protocols are often still active. Multi-factor authentication is available but not enforced. SharePoint external sharing may be open to anyone with a link. Admin accounts frequently share credentials with daily work accounts. None of this is Microsoft's fault — these defaults exist to simplify initial setup. But in a live business tenant, they represent real, exploitable gaps.

Small businesses with 5–50 employees are a primary target precisely because they are easier to exploit than enterprises while still carrying valuable data: client records, financial accounts, and supply chain access. A misconfigured Microsoft 365 tenant is the most common entry point in SMB breach cases we respond to.

This checklist covers Microsoft 365 Business Standard and Business Premium configurations. Where a specific control requires Business Premium (or an Entra ID P1 add-on), we note it clearly so you can make informed licensing decisions.

What Secure Score Should a Small Business Target?

Microsoft Secure Score is a built-in dashboard in the Microsoft 365 Defender portal that measures how well your tenant is configured relative to Microsoft's recommendations. It gives you a number and — more usefully — a prioritized list of exactly what to fix. Every unconfigured control costs you points. Every policy you enable earns them back.

Use it as a starting benchmark, not a final grade. A high score does not guarantee security, but a low score reliably predicts gaps that attackers exploit. Access it at security.microsoft.com → Secure Score.

Under 30%
High risk. Critical controls are missing. Immediate action required.
30–60%
Typical unmanaged tenant. Significant gaps likely present.
60–80%
Strong baseline. Target range for most SMB tenants.
80%+
Well-secured environment. Suitable for regulated industries.

Review your Secure Score monthly. Use the Improvement Actions tab to see a prioritized remediation list — each action shows the point value, difficulty, and where to configure it.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Enforcing MFA across every Microsoft 365 account is the single highest-impact control available to a small business. Passwords alone are routinely stolen through phishing, credential stuffing, and info-stealer malware. MFA blocks the overwhelming majority of automated account takeover attempts — and its absence is the most common reason cyber insurance claims are denied after a breach.

Enforce MFA on all user accounts

Why it matters: A stolen password alone is sufficient to access email, SharePoint, Teams, and all connected applications without MFA. Automated credential stuffing attacks test millions of stolen passwords daily.

Risk if skipped: Account takeover, business email compromise, and full mailbox access. Insurers frequently deny claims when MFA was not enforced at the time of the incident.

Where to configure: Microsoft Entra admin centre → Identity → Authentication methods → Policies. For simpler deployments, enable Security Defaults (Entra admin centre → Properties → Manage Security Defaults).

License: All Microsoft 365 plans. Per-user MFA is free; Conditional Access-enforced MFA requires Business Premium or Entra ID P1.

Prefer authenticator apps over SMS codes

Why it matters: SMS-based MFA is vulnerable to SIM swapping and call forwarding attacks. Authenticator app codes are generated locally and are not interception-prone.

Risk if skipped: A SIM swap attack allows an attacker to intercept the SMS code and bypass MFA entirely — a documented attack pattern in Canadian business email compromise cases.

Where to configure: Microsoft Entra admin centre → Authentication methods → Policies. Enable Microsoft Authenticator, disable SMS sign-in where possible.

License: All plans. Microsoft Authenticator is free.

Audit service accounts and shared mailboxes for MFA gaps

Why it matters: Shared mailboxes and service accounts frequently slip through MFA enforcement because they are not standard user accounts. Attackers specifically scan for these exclusions.

Where to configure: Microsoft Entra admin centre → Sign-in logs → Filter by Client App and look for Basic Auth, IMAP, POP. Review any account with MFA disabled or excluded from Conditional Access scope.

License: All plans.

Disable Legacy Authentication

Legacy authentication protocols — IMAP, POP3, basic SMTP auth, and older Exchange ActiveSync connections — cannot enforce MFA. A stolen password alone grants mailbox access through these protocols regardless of what MFA policies are set elsewhere in the tenant. Microsoft's own telemetry links legacy authentication to over 99% of password spray incidents against Microsoft 365 tenants.

Block legacy authentication tenant-wide

Why it matters: Legacy protocols bypass MFA completely. This is one of the highest-impact, lowest-effort changes you can make to a Microsoft 365 tenant.

Risk if skipped: Password-spray and credential stuffing attacks succeed reliably against tenants with legacy auth enabled. Your MFA policies offer no protection through these endpoints.

Where to configure: Microsoft Entra admin centre → Security → Conditional Access → New policy blocking "Other clients" and legacy authentication client apps. Security Defaults (free) block legacy auth automatically if Conditional Access is not in use.

License: Security Defaults — all plans, free. Conditional Access-based blocking — Business Premium or Entra ID P1.

Audit for active legacy auth connections before disabling

Why it matters: Some business applications — older multifunction printers, accounting software, third-party integrations — use legacy auth. Disabling without auditing first may break these connections.

Where to configure: Microsoft Entra admin centre → Sign-in logs → Filter by Client App → look for entries showing Basic Authentication, IMAP, POP, or SMTP. Identify and modernize any legitimate systems before enforcing the block.

License: All plans.

Conditional Access Policies

Conditional Access is the policy engine that controls how, from where, and from what devices your team can sign into Microsoft 365. It lets you enforce MFA on every sign-in, block logins from countries where you do not operate, and require compliant devices for admin actions. For a 5–50 person business, a small set of well-configured policies delivers significant protection beyond what Security Defaults alone provides.

Licensing note: Conditional Access requires Microsoft 365 Business Premium or Entra ID P1. If you are on Business Standard, Security Defaults are the free alternative — they enforce MFA and block legacy auth but do not support custom policies.

Require MFA for all users

Why it matters: A Conditional Access-enforced MFA policy applies to every sign-in automatically — including new accounts, app registrations, and guest access — with no per-user configuration required.

Where to configure: Microsoft Entra admin centre → Protection → Conditional Access → New policy. Assign to "All users", all cloud apps, grant access requiring "Multi-factor authentication". Exclude emergency break-glass accounts.

License: Business Premium or Entra ID P1.

Require compliant or hybrid-joined devices for admin actions

Why it matters: Admin actions performed from unmanaged personal devices are a high-risk vector. Requiring an Intune-enrolled compliant device adds a hardware-level gate that is very difficult to bypass remotely.

Where to configure: Conditional Access → New policy → Assign to Admin role directory roles → Grant: Require "Compliant device" or "Hybrid Azure AD joined".

License: Business Premium. Intune enrollment required.

Block sign-ins from countries you do not operate in

Why it matters: The majority of automated attacks against SMB tenants originate from overseas IP ranges. Geo-blocking sign-ins from countries where you have no employees or customers eliminates a large category of opportunistic attacks.

Where to configure: Conditional Access → Named Locations → Create an allowed list of countries → Block all other locations.

License: Business Premium or Entra ID P1.

Admin Role Protection

Global administrators have unrestricted access to your entire Microsoft 365 environment — including the ability to reset any password, delete data, remove audit logs, and modify security policies. Compromise of a single global admin account hands an attacker complete control of your tenant. Protecting these accounts is disproportionately important relative to the effort required.

Create dedicated admin accounts for administrative tasks

Why it matters: Using your regular work email account for admin tasks means a successful phishing attack on your daily inbox simultaneously captures global admin credentials.

Risk if skipped: An attacker who compromises your email account immediately has global admin access with no additional steps needed.

Where to configure: Microsoft Entra admin centre → Users → New user. Create admin-only accounts with no mailbox, not used for daily work, with a naming convention that identifies them as admin accounts (e.g., admin@domain.com).

License: All plans.

Protect global admin accounts with hardware security keys

Why it matters: Standard MFA (push notifications, TOTP codes) can be bypassed by adversary-in-the-middle phishing kits that steal authenticated session tokens. FIDO2 hardware keys are phishing-resistant — they cannot be intercepted remotely.

Risk if skipped: AiTM phishing kits routinely bypass push-notification and TOTP MFA for admin accounts. A hardware FIDO2 key stops this attack class entirely.

Where to configure: Microsoft Entra admin centre → Security → Authentication methods → FIDO2 security keys → Enable for admin accounts. Register a minimum of two physical keys per admin account as backup.

License: All plans for enabling FIDO2. Conditional Access enforcement requires Business Premium.

Apply least privilege — replace Global Admin with scoped roles where possible

Why it matters: Most IT staff and managed providers do not need global admin rights for day-to-day work. Limiting roles limits the blast radius if an admin account is compromised.

Where to configure: Microsoft Entra admin centre → Roles and admins → Review current global admin assignments. Replace with scoped roles: Exchange Administrator, SharePoint Administrator, Helpdesk Administrator as appropriate.

License: All plans.

Anti-Phishing Policies

Phishing is the leading cause of Microsoft 365 account compromise in small businesses. Microsoft Defender for Office 365 includes anti-phishing capabilities that go well beyond basic spam filtering — but they require configuration to be effective. A default tenant has minimal protection. A configured tenant has impersonation detection, mailbox intelligence, and first-contact safety tips active.

Licensing note: Enhanced anti-phishing (impersonation protection, mailbox intelligence) is part of Defender for Office 365 Plan 1, which is included in Microsoft 365 Business Premium. It is not available in Business Standard without a paid add-on.

Configure anti-phishing policies in Microsoft Defender

Why it matters: Default anti-phishing policies use conservative settings. Custom policies let you enable impersonation protection for your executives and domain, mailbox intelligence, and spoof detection tuned to your sending patterns.

Risk if skipped: CEO impersonation attacks, vendor fraud, and Microsoft login spoofs bypass standard spam filters because they contain no malware — only text and a convincing sender address.

Where to configure: Microsoft 365 Defender portal (security.microsoft.com) → Email & collaboration → Policies & rules → Threat policies → Anti-phishing. Enable impersonation protection for key users, set action to Quarantine, enable first-contact safety tips and unauthenticated sender indicators.

License: Business Premium (includes Defender for Office 365 Plan 1).

Safe Links and Safe Attachments

Safe Links rewrites URLs in emails and Office documents and checks them at the moment a user clicks — protecting against links that were safe at delivery but later redirected to a malicious site. Safe Attachments detonates suspicious email attachments in an isolated sandbox environment before delivery, blocking malware that signature-based scanning misses.

Enable Safe Links for email and Office 365 apps

Why it matters: Phishing links are often delivered before the malicious redirect is activated. Safe Links checks the destination at click time, not delivery time — closing the window that traditional URL scanning leaves open.

Risk if skipped: Time-delayed malicious redirects — a common technique to evade gateway scanning — are not detected without click-time verification.

Where to configure: Microsoft 365 Defender portal → Threat policies → Safe Links. Enable for email messages and Microsoft 365 apps. Enable "Do not allow users to click through to original URL" to prevent bypass.

License: Business Premium (Defender for Office 365 Plan 1).

Enable Safe Attachments for email, SharePoint, OneDrive, and Teams

Why it matters: Safe Attachments detonates suspicious files in an isolated environment before delivering them to the user's inbox. Novel malware that has never been seen before — which signature-based AV misses — is detected through behavioral analysis during detonation.

Risk if skipped: Zero-day malware delivered as email attachments is the most common ransomware delivery method and passes through signature-based scanning undetected.

Where to configure: Microsoft 365 Defender portal → Threat policies → Safe Attachments. Set action to Block. Enable Safe Attachments for SharePoint, OneDrive, and Microsoft Teams as well — malicious files shared through these channels bypass email scanning entirely.

License: Business Premium (Defender for Office 365 Plan 1).

DKIM and DMARC Configuration

SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are DNS-level email authentication standards. Together they prove that an email claiming to come from your domain was actually sent by an authorized server — and instruct receiving mail servers what to do with messages that fail the check. Without all three configured, anyone can send convincing spoofed email appearing to come from your domain.

Configure DKIM for your domain

Why it matters: DKIM adds a cryptographic signature to every outbound message. Receiving servers verify the signature, confirming the email originated from your domain and was not altered in transit.

Risk if skipped: Without DKIM, spoofed emails appearing to come from your domain can pass authentication checks at receiving mail servers. Your domain's sending reputation is also more vulnerable to abuse by third parties.

Where to configure: Microsoft 365 Defender portal → Email & collaboration → Policies & rules → Threat policies → Email authentication settings → DKIM tab. Enable DKIM for each custom domain, then add the two CNAME records provided to your DNS registrar.

License: All plans. DKIM signing is a standard Exchange Online feature.

Publish and enforce a DMARC policy

Why it matters: DMARC builds on SPF and DKIM — it defines what receiving servers should do when email from your domain fails authentication: ignore it (p=none), quarantine it (p=quarantine), or reject it outright (p=reject). A DMARC policy of p=reject is the strongest protection against domain spoofing.

Risk if skipped: Spoofed emails pretending to be from your company are delivered to your clients and partners with no warnings. Business email compromise and invoice fraud attacks frequently exploit domains with no DMARC policy.

Where to configure: Add a DMARC TXT record to your domain's DNS at your registrar. Start with p=none and a reporting address to monitor failures for 2–4 weeks, then move to p=quarantine, then p=reject once all legitimate mail sources are confirmed passing.

License: No Microsoft license required — configured entirely at the DNS level.

Mailbox Auditing

Mailbox auditing records a log of actions taken in each user's mailbox — who accessed it, what was read, what was deleted, and whether forwarding rules were added. Without it, a post-breach investigation cannot determine what data was accessed or whether an attacker is still silently reading your email through a forwarding rule.

Verify mailbox auditing is enabled tenant-wide

Why it matters: Mailbox auditing is enabled by default for accounts created after January 2019 — but older accounts and service accounts may not have it active. Audit logs are the primary evidence source in any incident investigation.

Risk if skipped: After a compromise, there are no logs to determine which emails were accessed, what data was read, or whether forwarding rules were created to copy outbound mail to an attacker's address.

Where to configure: Exchange admin centre → Mailboxes → review AuditEnabled property. Or run via PowerShell: Get-EXOMailbox -ResultSize Unlimited | Select-Object UserPrincipalName, AuditEnabled

License: All plans. Exchange Online Audit is included.

Review all mailboxes for unauthorized forwarding rules

Why it matters: After a mailbox is compromised, attackers commonly add inbox rules that silently forward copies of all incoming email to an external address. These rules persist even after the account password is reset.

Risk if skipped: Undetected forwarding rules can exfiltrate months of correspondence after the initial breach is believed resolved. This is one of the most common post-compromise persistence mechanisms we encounter.

Where to configure: Microsoft 365 Defender portal → Explorer → Search for inbox rules with external forwarding. Exchange admin centre → Mail flow → Remote Domains and transport rules. Review quarterly.

License: All plans.

External Sharing and Guest Access Controls

By default, Microsoft 365 tenants allow broad external sharing in SharePoint and OneDrive. Users can share any file with anyone using a link that requires no sign-in. Guest access in Teams and SharePoint, if not periodically reviewed, can leave former contractors or clients with active access to internal channels and documents long after the relationship ended.

Restrict SharePoint and OneDrive external sharing

Why it matters: "Anyone with the link" sharing means files are accessible by anyone who receives the URL — no authentication, no audit trail, no expiry unless explicitly set. A single accidentally shared link can expose sensitive client documents publicly.

Risk if skipped: Confidential files can be shared publicly through careless link forwarding or phishing attacks that extract sharing URLs from compromised email accounts.

Where to configure: Microsoft 365 admin centre → SharePoint admin centre → Policies → Sharing. Set external sharing to "New and existing guests" (requires authentication) or "Only people in your organization" for maximum restriction. Set default link expiry and require re-authentication for external links.

License: All plans.

Review and expire guest accounts quarterly

Why it matters: Guest accounts added for a project or contractor relationship often persist long after the engagement ends, retaining access to Teams channels and SharePoint libraries containing current client data.

Risk if skipped: Departed contractors with active guest accounts represent both a data leak risk and a potential entry point — especially if their personal email account is later compromised.

Where to configure: Microsoft Entra admin centre → External identities → All guests. Review active guest accounts and remove any that no longer require access. Automated access reviews are available with Business Premium.

License: All plans for manual review. Automated access review policies require Business Premium or Entra ID P2.

Audit Logging and Alert Policies

Unified audit logging captures activity across Exchange, SharePoint, Teams, and Entra ID into a single searchable record. Alert policies trigger notifications when specific high-risk events occur — a mass file download, an unusual number of failed sign-ins, or a forwarding rule added to a mailbox. Together they are the foundation of any incident detection capability in a Microsoft 365 environment.

Enable unified audit logging

Why it matters: Audit logs are essential for incident investigation and are increasingly reviewed by cyber insurers as evidence of a monitoring capability. Without them, forensic investigation after a breach is blind.

Risk if skipped: You cannot determine what was accessed, by whom, or when. Incident response becomes guesswork, and cyber insurance claims may be harder to substantiate.

Where to configure: Microsoft Purview compliance portal → Audit → Verify "Recording user and admin activity" is On. Default retention is 90 days; Business Premium extends this to 1 year.

License: All plans. 1-year audit retention requires Business Premium or Microsoft 365 E3.

Configure alert policies for high-risk events

Why it matters: Default alert policies cover some scenarios but miss business-specific risks. Custom policies alert when large numbers of files are downloaded, forwarding rules are created, or admin rights are elevated — giving you early warning before significant damage occurs.

Risk if skipped: Breaches persist for weeks or months in tenants without active alerting. Attackers slowly exfiltrate data through channels that generate no default notifications.

Where to configure: Microsoft 365 Defender portal → Alerts → Alert policies. Review existing defaults and create custom policies for: mass file download from SharePoint, mailbox forwarding rule creation, admin role elevation, and multiple failed sign-ins from a single account.

License: Default policies — all plans. Custom alert policies — Business Premium or Microsoft 365 E3.

Staff Phishing Awareness Training

Technical controls reduce the attack surface considerably — but a convincing phishing email still reaches inboxes. Staff training is the last line of defence and the most frequently skipped one. The goal is not to eliminate all clicks; it is to make suspicious emails reportable, normalize asking for help, and ensure every employee knows what to do in the first 15 minutes after clicking something suspicious.

  • Run short monthly phishing simulations using Microsoft Attack Simulator (included in Business Premium) or a third-party tool — frequency matters more than session length
  • Install the Microsoft "Report Message" Outlook add-in for all users — reporting should be a single click, not a process
  • Make it explicitly safe to report a click without fear of blame — a reported incident is far easier to contain than a hidden one
  • Teach staff to recognize the three most common patterns: invoice fraud, Microsoft 365 login spoofs, and CEO or executive impersonation
  • Document a clear procedure for what to do immediately after clicking a suspicious link: who to call, whether to close the browser, whether to shut down the device

Endpoint Security for Business Devices

Endpoints — laptops, desktops, phones, and tablets, including personal devices used for work — are the other primary attack surface alongside email. Built-in Windows Defender provides a baseline but is not sufficient for a business with remote workers, BYOD devices, or any active cyber insurance policy. Microsoft Defender for Business (included in Business Premium) provides EDR-level protection managed centrally through Intune.

Deploy endpoint detection and response (EDR) on all devices

Why it matters: Traditional antivirus matches known signatures. EDR monitors device behaviour, detects suspicious activity patterns, isolates compromised machines, and supports investigation. Most cyber insurers now expect EDR, not legacy antivirus.

Where to configure: Microsoft Defender for Business is included in Business Premium and deploys through Intune. For tenants on Business Standard, Defender for Business is available as a standalone SMB add-on.

License: Included in Business Premium. Available as a standalone add-on for Business Standard tenants.

Enforce disk encryption and automatic OS updates via Intune

Why it matters: An unencrypted laptop lost or stolen exposes all locally cached files and credentials. Unpatched operating systems are the primary exploitation target for opportunistic attacks seeking an entry point beyond email.

Where to configure: Microsoft Intune (Endpoint Manager) → Devices → Configuration profiles. Enforce BitLocker encryption, require Windows Update for Business, configure update deferral windows.

License: Intune is included in Business Premium.

Implement a written BYOD policy with defined access requirements

Why it matters: Personal devices accessing business email or SharePoint fall outside standard management. Without a policy, you have no visibility or control over the security posture of devices that hold company data.

Where to configure: Intune app protection policies enforce encryption, PIN requirements, and selective data wipe on personal devices without requiring full device enrollment. Define minimum OS version and screen-lock requirements.

License: Business Premium or Intune standalone.

Microsoft 365 Backup — Retention Is Not a Backup

Microsoft's shared responsibility model is explicit: Microsoft protects the platform. Your data is your responsibility. The Recycle Bin, OneDrive version history, and SharePoint retention policies are convenience recovery features — not backups. They have short retention windows, can be emptied by a compromised admin account, and do not protect you from ransomware encryption, insider deletion, a departed employee, or tenant misconfiguration.

Deploy a third-party Microsoft 365 backup solution

Why it matters: A real backup is an independent, immutable copy of Exchange, SharePoint, OneDrive, and Teams data — stored outside the production tenant and restorable to any point in time, including before an incident occurred.

Risk if skipped: Ransomware encryption of a Microsoft 365 tenant, an admin account deletion, or an employee departing with their account leaves you with no recovery path beyond what Microsoft's short-window retention preserves — which is frequently not enough.

Where to configure: Third-party solutions (Veeam, Acronis, Datto, Dropsuite) integrate with Microsoft 365 via the Graph API and store encrypted backups in a separate cloud tenancy with configurable retention periods.

License: Not included in any Microsoft 365 plan. Third-party backup is a separate service. Budget for it accordingly.

Test backup restores quarterly and document results

Why it matters: An untested backup is not a backup. Backup software can fail silently, API permissions can expire, and retention policies may not capture what you believe they do. Quarterly restore tests confirm your recovery capability before you need it.

How to test: Restore a sample mailbox, a SharePoint library folder, and a OneDrive folder to a test location. Verify timestamp accuracy, file completeness, and restore time. Document the results for your cyber insurance renewal record.

License: Depends on your third-party backup solution.

Ransomware Protection and Incident Response

Ransomware attacks on small businesses almost always follow the same path: a phishing email compromises a credential, the attacker uses that identity to move laterally, then deploys encryption across reachable storage. Every stage of that chain is breakable — which is why layered controls matter more than any single tool. MFA stops credential-only attacks. Endpoint protection detects lateral movement. Safe Attachments blocks malicious payloads at delivery. Backup provides a recovery path if everything else fails.

Recovery planning comes down to two numbers: how much data you can afford to lose (Recovery Point Objective) and how long you can afford to be offline (Recovery Time Objective). Most small businesses discover those numbers are far smaller than assumed when they are inside an active incident.

  • Enforce least-privilege access on both user and admin accounts — limit the blast radius of any single compromised identity
  • Apply the 3-2-1 rule: three copies of your data, two different media types, one copy outside the production cloud tenant
  • Test restores quarterly and document the results — an untested backup is not a backup
  • Write down who makes decisions and who communicates in the first 60 minutes of an incident, before you need it
  • Confirm your cyber insurance policy's position on ransom payment in advance — some policies require insurer authorization before any payment

Common Microsoft 365 Security Gaps We Find in Small Business Tenants

These are the issues we encounter most frequently when we audit a new client's Microsoft 365 tenant. None require expensive tools to fix — they require configuration and attention.

  • Shared admin credentials. Multiple staff — or a managed IT provider — using a single shared global admin account created during initial setup and never reviewed. Shared accounts have no individual accountability, cannot be audited per-user, and cannot have MFA applied cleanly.
  • MFA gaps on service and shared accounts. MFA is enforced for standard users, but service accounts and shared mailboxes were excluded from the Conditional Access scope. Attackers scan specifically for these exclusions as the path of least resistance.
  • Legacy authentication still active. Often because an older printer, photocopier, or accounting software integration was never modernized. The business believes MFA is protecting the tenant — but legacy auth protocols bypass MFA entirely for those connections.
  • SharePoint sharing set to "Anyone." Default or misconfigured sharing settings mean files can be shared via a link with no authentication required, no expiry, and no audit trail. Confidential documents end up with publicly accessible URLs.
  • No mailbox auditing review. Auditing was never verified on older accounts. After a compromise, there are no logs to determine which emails were accessed, what was forwarded, or when the attacker first gained access.
  • DKIM and DMARC not configured. SPF is set up but DKIM and DMARC were never configured. Attackers spoof the business domain to run invoice fraud and impersonation campaigns directly against the company's clients and suppliers.
  • No third-party backup. The business assumes Microsoft backs up their data. Version history and the Recycle Bin are the assumed recovery tools — until a ransomware event or admin deletion leaves nothing recoverable within the 90-day retention window.
  • Guest accounts never reviewed. Contractors and project collaborators added as guests 12–18 months ago still have active access to Teams channels and SharePoint libraries containing current client data. Nobody owns the review process.

If any of these describe your tenant, the detailed sections earlier in this guide tell you exactly where to configure each fix — with licensing notes included.
